## **1. Introduction: The Enterprise Spreadsheet Evolution**

I need to address what makes Smartsheet fundamentally different from every other tool in this review space right away. Smartsheet isn't trying to be the trendiest project management tool. It's not chasing Monday.com's visual appeal or Trello's simplicity. Instead, Smartsheet built something that enterprise organizations desperately needed: a spreadsheet that actually understands project management.

After managing over 40 projects, configuring complex resource allocations, building executive dashboards, and pushing the automation engine across a 14-person team spanning operations, IT infrastructure, and marketing, I can tell you exactly where Smartsheet delivers genuine enterprise value and where it shows its age. The platform occupies a unique position in the market—it's simultaneously the most powerful project scheduling tool I've tested and one of the least enjoyable to use daily.

The critical insight I gained during six months of testing: Smartsheet isn't competing with Trello or even Monday.com. It's competing with spreadsheet chaos—the millions of Excel files masquerading as project management systems across enterprises worldwide. Understanding this positioning explains both Smartsheet's strengths and its limitations. If your organization runs on spreadsheets and needs real project management capabilities without abandoning the familiar grid format, Smartsheet is the answer. If your team prefers visual boards and modern interfaces, keep looking.

My testing framework evaluates project management tools across ease of use, feature depth, enterprise capabilities, support quality, value for money, scalability, and team adoption rate. Smartsheet scored remarkably high in Gantt chart capabilities, resource management, and enterprise features, but struggled with modern UX expectations, pricing transparency, and the adoption rate among team members who expected a more contemporary interface.

## **2. What is Smartsheet? Understanding the Platform**

\[VISUAL: Company timeline infographic showing Smartsheet's growth from 2005 to present\]

Smartsheet launched in 2005, making it ancient by SaaS standards—predating Asana, Trello, Monday.com, and ClickUp by years. Founded by Brent Frei and Mark Mader in Bellevue, Washington, the company emerged from a simple observation: organizations were managing projects in spreadsheets because nothing better existed that felt familiar. Rather than creating something entirely new, the founders asked: "What if spreadsheets could actually manage projects?"

Today, Smartsheet serves over 10 million users including 90% of Fortune 100 companies. The company went public in 2018 (NYSE: SMAR) with a market capitalization exceeding $7 billion at its peak. These enterprise adoption metrics matter—they indicate that despite the dated interface, Smartsheet handles demands that consumer-focused tools cannot.

The platform positions itself squarely in the enterprise work management space. While Monday.com emphasizes visual boards and Asana focuses on task coordination, Smartsheet targets complex project and portfolio management that large organizations require. Everything looks and feels familiar to Excel users. Rows represent tasks or items. Columns define fields. Formulas calculate values. Cell references work similarly. This familiarity accelerates adoption in spreadsheet-native organizations—which describes most enterprises.

The core architecture uses sheets as the fundamental building block. Sheets contain rows (tasks or items) and columns (fields), and rows can have parent-child hierarchy for work breakdown structures. Views transform the same data into Grid, Gantt, Card, or Calendar formats. Dashboards aggregate data from multiple sheets into visual summaries. Reports pull information across sheets and workspaces for cross-project analysis. And Control Center manages project portfolios at scale with templated project creation and rollup reporting.

## **3. Smartsheet Pricing & Plans: The Enterprise Premium**

Smartsheet's pricing is where most evaluations get complicated, because the sticker price dramatically understates the actual cost. No free plan exists—only a 30-day trial. And the add-on structure means the base price is just the starting point.

### **3.1 Pro Plan ($9/user/month) - The Starting Point**

\[SCREENSHOT: Pro plan showing basic sheets and views\]

At $9 per user monthly (billed annually), the Pro plan provides the core spreadsheet-meets-project-management experience. You get unlimited sheets, Grid and Gantt views, basic automation, forms for data collection, and the ability to share sheets with external collaborators.

During our evaluation, we started three team members on the Pro plan to test base capabilities. The core grid and Gantt functionality worked well, but we hit walls quickly. The automation limit (250 runs per month per sheet) constrained our workflow automation. No resource management, no Control Center, and no advanced reporting meant the Pro plan felt like a capable spreadsheet with a timeline view rather than a full project management system.

**Reality Check:** Pro is viable for small teams (under 10 people) managing straightforward projects. The moment you need resource allocation, portfolio management, or substantive automation, you'll upgrade to Business.

### **3.2 Business Plan ($19/user/month) - Where Smartsheet Gets Serious**

\[SCREENSHOT: Business plan showing resource management and Control Center\]

At $19 per user monthly (billed annually), the Business plan unlocks the features that justify Smartsheet's enterprise positioning. Resource management lets you track team allocation across projects. This is where our 14-person team operated for most of the evaluation. The unlimited automation meant we could build substantive workflows without counting runs.

**Best For:** Teams of 10-50 people managing multiple concurrent projects with shared resources, and organizations needing executive-level portfolio visibility.

Enterprise adds Control Center (templated project creation and portfolio management), Smartsheet Advance (premium add-on suite), directory integration, SSO/SAML, and enterprise-grade governance. Pricing is custom and typically lands between $25-40 per user monthly depending on volume and add-ons.

## **4. Key Features Deep Dive**

### **4.1 Grid View & Sheets - The Spreadsheet Foundation**

\[SCREENSHOT: Grid view with conditional formatting, comments, and real-time collaboration indicators\]

Everything in Smartsheet starts with sheets, and the grid view is where you'll spend most of your time. It looks exactly like Excel or Google Sheets—rows represent items, columns define fields, cells contain data. The familiarity is immediate for anyone who's used spreadsheets, and that familiarity is Smartsheet's primary adoption advantage.

But the grid goes beyond static spreadsheets in ways that matter for project management. Real-time collaboration shows other users' activity in the sheet—you can see who's editing what, similar to Google Sheets. Comments attach to specific rows for contextual discussion, which replaces the "let me email you about row 47" pattern that plagues Excel-based project management. Cell history tracks every change with timestamps and user attribution, creating an audit trail that spreadsheets can't match.

The column types are where Smartsheet's project management DNA emerges from the spreadsheet shell. Beyond standard text, number, and date columns, Smartsheet offers Contact List columns that pull from your organization's directory, Duration columns that calculate in work days, and the critical Predecessor column that defines task dependencies with Finish-to-Start, Start-to-Start, Finish-to-Finish, and Start-to-Finish relationships including lag time. That Predecessor column is what transforms a spreadsheet into a project schedule—when Task A slips by two days, every dependent task adjusts automatically.

Row hierarchy creates work breakdown structures that project managers need. Indent rows to create parent-child relationships, and parent rows automatically summarize their children—dates roll up to show earliest start and latest end, percentages calculate weighted completion, and numbers sum as configured. We used three-level hierarchies (Phase > Task > Subtask) for our IT infrastructure projects, and the automatic rollup meant our project status dashboards always reflected current reality without manual updates.

**Pro Tip:** Cross-sheet formulas are Smartsheet's hidden superpower. You can reference data from other sheets in your formulas, which enables centralized dashboards that pull real-time data from dozens of project sheets. We built an executive dashboard that calculated portfolio health scores by referencing completion percentages, budget variance, and risk ratings from 15 different project sheets.

### **4.2 Gantt Charts - Where Smartsheet Outclasses the Competition**

\[SCREENSHOT: Gantt chart with dependencies, critical path highlighted, and baseline comparison\]

I'll state it directly: Smartsheet has the best Gantt chart implementation of any cloud-based project management tool I've tested. Not the prettiest—Monday.com and ClickUp have more visually appealing timelines—but the most functionally complete for serious project scheduling.

Toggle any sheet to Gantt view and tasks appear as bars on a timeline. Start and end dates define position. Duration determines length. Dependency lines connect related tasks. The critical path highlights in red, showing you which sequence of tasks determines the overall project duration. Drag a task bar to reschedule it, and all dependent tasks cascade automatically.

The critical path calculation is what separates Smartsheet from visual project management tools. Monday.com and Asana show timelines, but they don't calculate which tasks are critical—meaning any delay would push the project end date. During our IT infrastructure migration, the critical path analysis revealed that our database migration task (which we'd estimated at 5 days) was the bottleneck for the entire 8-week project. We allocated additional resources to that task, shortened it to 3 days, and the project end date moved up by 2 days. That kind of scheduling intelligence requires proper critical path analysis, and Smartsheet delivers it natively.

Baseline comparison is another enterprise feature that matters. Set a baseline snapshot of your project schedule, then compare actual progress against the plan. We set baselines at the start of each project phase and used the variance view during weekly status meetings. When a project phase was trending 4 days late, the baseline comparison made it visible in the first week rather than the last.

**What's Missing:** The Gantt chart rendering isn't as smooth as desktop tools like Microsoft Project or Primavera. Dragging task bars on large schedules (200+ tasks) can feel laggy. Color customization is limited—you can't create the detailed visual coding that construction PMs often want. And there's no built-in earned value management, which enterprise PMOs sometimes require.

### **4.3 Resource Management - Genuine Capacity Planning**

\[SCREENSHOT: Resource management heatmap showing team allocation across projects\]

Resource management is Smartsheet's most underappreciated feature and the one that most justified our investment. The Resource Management module (formerly 10,000ft, acquired by Smartsheet in 2019) provides genuine capacity planning that most project management tools don't attempt.

The heatmap view shows each team member's allocation across all projects on a timeline. Green means available capacity, yellow means fully allocated, and red means over-allocated. During our testing, this view revealed something we'd suspected but couldn't prove: our senior architect was allocated to 140% capacity across three concurrent projects. No one had noticed because the allocations lived in separate project sheets. The resource heatmap made the conflict immediately visible.

We used resource management to plan our quarterly project portfolio. Before committing to new projects, we could see which team members had capacity and which were already at their limits. This prevented the common enterprise problem of committing to more projects than the team can realistically staff—a problem we'd been solving with gut feel and spreadsheets before Smartsheet.

**Reality Check:** The Resource Management add-on costs an additional $12/user/month on top of the Business plan. For our 14-person team, that added $168/month to an already substantial bill. It's worth it for teams managing 5+ concurrent projects with shared resources—the visibility alone prevents costly over-commitment. For teams with dedicated project staff and no resource contention, it's unnecessary.

### **4.4 Automation & Workflows**

\[SCREENSHOT: Automation builder showing a multi-condition approval workflow\]

Smartsheet's automation engine handles the repetitive work that makes spreadsheet-based project management tedious. The builder uses a trigger-condition-action model: when something happens (trigger), check if conditions are met (condition), then do something (action).

We built 28 automation rules across our project sheets. The most impactful was an approval workflow for project change requests. When a team member submitted a change request via a Smartsheet form, the automation routed it to the project manager for review, then to the sponsor for approval if the budget impact exceeded $5,000, and finally updated the project plan with the approved changes. Before this automation, change requests took 3-5 days to process through email chains. After automation, the average dropped to 6 hours.

Status-based automations saved the most cumulative time. When a task's status changed to "Complete," the automation updated the parent row's progress, notified the dependent task owner, and moved the next task to "Ready." These small automations, running across 40 project sheets, eliminated hundreds of manual updates per week.

The automation limitations are notable. The Pro plan's 250 runs per month per sheet is insufficient for active projects—we burned through that in the first week. The logic builder doesn't support complex conditional branching (if-else-if chains). And there's no way to trigger automations from external events without using the API or a third-party tool like Zapier.

### **4.5 Dashboards & Reporting - Executive Visibility**

\[SCREENSHOT: Executive dashboard with portfolio health metrics, resource allocation chart, and project timeline\]

Dashboards transform Smartsheet's raw data into visual summaries that executives actually understand. You build dashboards by adding widgets that pull data from sheets, reports, and other dashboards—charts, metrics, tables, web content, and images.

Our executive dashboard became the single most valuable artifact in our Smartsheet deployment. It displayed five widgets: a portfolio health summary (showing green/yellow/red status for all active projects), a resource allocation heatmap, a budget variance chart, a milestone timeline showing upcoming deliverables across all projects, and an issues log filtered to high-priority items. The operations VP reviewed this dashboard every Monday morning and used it to make staffing and priority decisions. Before Smartsheet, generating this view required a half-day of manual data compilation from multiple spreadsheets.

Reports aggregate data from multiple sheets into unified views. We created a cross-project risk report that pulled all rows with "Risk" status from 15 project sheets into a single, sortable, filterable view. The report updated in real-time as teams updated their project sheets, eliminating the weekly "send me your risk register" email.

**What's Missing:** Dashboard design feels dated compared to modern BI tools. Widget customization is limited—you can't control colors, fonts, or layouts with the precision that tools like Power BI or Tableau provide. For organizations with serious analytics needs, Smartsheet dashboards serve as operational summaries, not analytical tools.

### **4.6 Control Center - Portfolio Management at Scale**

\[SCREENSHOT: Control Center showing project portfolio with templated creation and rollup metrics\]

Control Center is Smartsheet's enterprise portfolio management module, available on the Enterprise plan. It standardizes project delivery by creating new projects from templates and aggregating data across all projects in a portfolio.

We evaluated Control Center during a trial period, and the concept is powerful. Define a project template (including sheets, dashboards, and reports), and Control Center creates a complete new project workspace with one click. All naming conventions, column structures, and automation rules carry over. Portfolio-level rollups show metrics across all projects created from the template—total budget, aggregate completion, resource utilization.

For PMOs managing 20+ concurrent projects of similar type (construction projects, software implementations, consulting engagements), Control Center removes the inconsistency that plagues portfolio management. Every project follows the same structure, reports the same metrics, and feeds the same portfolio dashboard.

## **6. Smartsheet Cons: The Enterprise Tradeoffs**

\[VISUAL: Cons summary infographic\]

### **The Interface Feels Dated**

There's no diplomatic way to say it: Smartsheet looks like it was designed in 2012 and hasn't had a meaningful visual refresh since. The grid view is functional but uninspiring. The navigation is cluttered. The color palette is dull. When team members see Monday.com's vibrant boards or Linear's elegant interface, then look at Smartsheet's gray grids, enthusiasm drops visibly. Our marketing team described using Smartsheet as "working in a spreadsheet with extra steps," which isn't the reaction you want from a tool costing $19+/user/month.

### **Pricing Complexity Obscures True Cost**

The base price is misleading. Resource Management, premium connectors, Control Center, advanced Brandfolder features—each adds cost. Our actual per-user cost was roughly $300/month when you included all the pieces we needed, compared to the $19/user sticker price. The gap between advertised and actual cost is wider than any other tool in this comparison.

### **The Learning Curve for Non-Spreadsheet Users**

While spreadsheet-native users adopt quickly, team members without strong Excel skills face a steeper learning curve than modern visual tools require. The formula syntax, cross-sheet references, and automation builder assume comfort with structured data concepts. Our marketing team members who preferred visual tools like Trello took three weeks to become productive, compared to the three days they needed with Monday.com.

### **Mobile Experience Is Weak**

Smartsheet's mobile app handles viewing and basic editing, but it can't replace the desktop experience for any substantive work. Gantt charts are essentially unusable on phone screens. Complex sheet editing is frustrating with touch controls. Automation configuration requires a desktop. For teams that need mobile project management, Smartsheet isn't viable as a mobile-first tool.

### **Overkill for Simple Project Management**

If your project management needs are "track tasks, assign owners, set due dates," Smartsheet's complexity adds overhead without proportional value. The grid interface, column configuration, automation setup, and dashboard building require investment that simpler tools don't demand. Teams with straightforward needs should use Trello, Asana, or ClickUp instead.

### **Performance Can Lag on Large Sheets**

Sheets with 5,000+ rows and complex formulas show noticeable performance degradation. Gantt chart rendering slows with 200+ tasks and many dependencies. Cross-sheet formulas with large data sets can take several seconds to recalculate. For enterprise deployments with large, formula-heavy sheets, performance optimization becomes an ongoing concern.

**Caution:** Smartsheet's per-sheet automation limits on the Pro plan (250 runs/month) are almost certainly insufficient for active projects. Budget for the Business plan ($19/user/month) from the start if you plan to use automation meaningfully.

## **7. Setup & Implementation**

\[VISUAL: Implementation timeline infographic\]

### **The Real Timeline**

Smartsheet implementation takes longer than modern PM tools because there's more to configure. Here's what our six-month experience taught us.

**Weeks 1-2: Foundation**

Create your workspace structure, define column standards that all projects will follow, and build your first 2-3 project sheets. Import existing project data from Excel (Smartsheet handles this well—our 500-row project tracker imported cleanly with all formulas needing only minor adjustment). Connect integrations (we set up Slack, Google Drive, and Salesforce in the first week). Start with Grid view and basic Gantt to get the team comfortable.

**Weeks 3-4: Templates & Automation**

Build project templates that standardize your sheet structure, column types, and baseline automations. This is the most important investment—we created three templates (IT project, marketing campaign, operations initiative) that every subsequent project used. Build your first automation rules for the most painful manual processes. Train the team on column types, formulas, and basic views.

**Weeks 5-8: Dashboards & Integration**

Create executive dashboards that aggregate data from your active project sheets. Build cross-sheet reports for portfolio-level visibility. Configure resource management if you're on the Business plan. Set up form-based intake for project requests and change management.

**Weeks 9-12: Optimization**

Refine templates based on real usage. Build advanced automation rules. Optimize formula performance on large sheets. Train stakeholders on dashboard interpretation. Establish governance practices for sheet management.

**Pro Tip:** Invest in template design before anything else. A well-structured project template saves hours on every subsequent project and ensures consistent reporting across your portfolio. We spent two days building our IT project template and it's been used 25 times since—the ROI on those two days was enormous.

If your project management is "assign tasks and track completion," Smartsheet is dramatically overengineered for your needs. Todoist, Trello, or even a shared Google Sheet will serve you better with less overhead.

## **12. Customer Support Reality Check**

Our support experience was adequate but not exceptional on the Business plan. We submitted 8 support tickets over six months. Response times averaged 16 hours for standard issues—competent responses with step-by-step solutions. Two complex automation issues required escalation and took 4-5 days to resolve.

## **13. Performance & Reliability**

\[VISUAL: Performance metrics\]

Smartsheet's performance is generally reliable for standard workloads but degrades noticeably with scale. Sheets with under 2,000 rows and moderate formulas perform well—page loads under 2 seconds, responsive editing, smooth Gantt chart interaction. Sheets exceeding 5,000 rows with complex cross-sheet formulas show lag—formula recalculation takes 3-5 seconds, Gantt chart scrolling stutters, and bulk operations slow down.

We experienced no full platform outages during our six-month evaluation. Two brief periods of degraded performance (slower-than-normal load times) lasted approximately 20 minutes each. The status page at status.smartsheet.com provided transparent communication during both incidents.

The mobile app performs adequately for viewing and basic editing. Gantt charts on mobile are essentially read-only due to screen size constraints.

### **Is Smartsheet just a fancy spreadsheet?**

It started that way, but it's evolved well beyond spreadsheets. Gantt charts with critical path analysis, resource management, portfolio dashboards, workflow automation, and form-based data collection go far beyond what any spreadsheet can do. ### **How does Smartsheet compare to Microsoft Project?**

Smartsheet provides about 80% of Microsoft Project's scheduling capability at significantly lower cost, with dramatically better collaboration features. Microsoft Project is more powerful for megaproject scheduling (earned value management, advanced resource leveling), but Smartsheet is more accessible and collaborative. Most organizations under 500 people should choose Smartsheet over Project.

### **Is Smartsheet worth it for small teams?**

Probably not. Without a free plan and with practical costs of $19-40+/user/month, small teams (under 10 people) get better value from ClickUp, Asana, or Monday.com. Smartsheet's enterprise features provide little value at small scale.

### **Can Smartsheet replace Excel for project management?**

Yes, and that's exactly what it's designed to do. The grid interface feels familiar to Excel users, formulas use similar syntax, and the learning curve is gentle for spreadsheet-native teams. The addition of real-time collaboration, Gantt charts, automation, and resource management makes it a meaningful upgrade from Excel-based project tracking.

### **Does Smartsheet have a free plan?**

No. Smartsheet offers a 30-day free trial. After that, the cheapest plan is Pro at $9/user/month (billed annually). For individual use, Smartsheet offers a limited free tier for a single user, but it's not practical for team use.

### **How does Smartsheet handle dependencies?**

Smartsheet supports all four dependency types: Finish-to-Start, Start-to-Start, Finish-to-Finish, and Start-to-Finish, with configurable lag and lead time. Dependencies are defined in the Predecessor column and visualized as connecting lines on the Gantt chart. When predecessor tasks move, dependent tasks cascade automatically.

### **Is Smartsheet HIPAA compliant?**

Yes, on the Enterprise plan with a Business Associate Agreement. This makes Smartsheet suitable for healthcare organizations managing projects that involve protected health information.

### **Is Smartsheet FedRAMP authorized?**

Yes, at the Moderate impact level. Smartsheet also offers a GovCloud environment for US government agencies. This distinguishes it from most competitors in the PM space.

### **Can I use Smartsheet on mobile?**

Yes, iOS and Android apps are available. The mobile experience handles viewing, basic editing, and approvals well. Complex operations (Gantt chart editing, formula writing, automation configuration) require the desktop experience. Smartsheet is not a mobile-first tool.

### **How does Smartsheet's resource management work?**

The Resource Management module (add-on, $12/user/month on Business plan) shows team member allocation across all projects on a heatmap. You can see who's available, who's over-committed, and plan assignments based on actual capacity. It was formerly a standalone product called 10,000ft that Smartsheet acquired in 2019.

### **What's Smartsheet Control Center?**

An Enterprise-tier feature for portfolio management. It creates new projects from standardized templates and aggregates metrics across all projects in a portfolio. Essential for PMOs managing 10+ concurrent projects of similar type.

### **Can Smartsheet integrate with other tools?**

Yes, through native integrations (Salesforce, Jira, Slack, Microsoft Teams, Google Workspace, Tableau), premium connectors (additional cost), and the Smartsheet API. Zapier and Make also connect Smartsheet to 2,000+ additional services.
