## **1. Introduction: The Help Desk That Defined the Category**

Zendesk is to customer support what Salesforce is to CRM—the platform that defined the category and remains the standard against which everything else is measured. After five months running Zendesk with a 15-person support team handling 8,000+ tickets monthly across email, chat, phone, and social media, I understand both why Zendesk dominates enterprise support and why newer tools like Intercom are capturing the market's imagination.

Zendesk does everything. Ticketing with sophisticated routing rules and SLA management. Live chat with AI-powered chatbot suggestions. Phone support with IVR, call recording, and callback functionality. Knowledge base with community forums and content analytics. Customer satisfaction surveys with trend reporting. Reporting with custom dashboards and cross-channel analytics. Integrations with 1,500+ marketplace apps. Multi-brand support for organizations managing multiple customer-facing brands. Multi-language support for global operations. The breadth is unmatched in the customer support space—and that breadth is both Zendesk's greatest strength and the source of its greatest criticism: complexity.

After managing 40,000+ tickets through the platform, I can tell you that Zendesk handles enterprise support complexity better than any alternative. The routing rules ensure every ticket reaches the right agent. The SLA management holds the team accountable to response and resolution targets. The trigger-based automation eliminates manual repetitive work. And the multi-channel Agent Workspace creates a unified experience where an agent handles email, chat, and phone from a single interface without switching tools.

But I can also tell you that reaching that level of operational excellence required three weeks of dedicated configuration, ongoing administrative attention, and a complexity level that simpler tools don't demand. The out-of-box experience is functional but generic—Zendesk's power comes from customization, and customization requires investment.

Zendesk was founded in 2007 by Mikkel Svane, Alexander Aghassipour, and Morten Primdahl in Copenhagen (later relocated to San Francisco). The company went public in 2014, reached $1.6 billion in annual revenue, and was taken private by Hellman & Friedman and Permira in 2022 for $10.2 billion. Today, Zendesk serves over 100,000 businesses across every industry.

My testing framework evaluates customer support platforms across ticketing depth, multi-channel coverage, AI capabilities, SLA management, reporting quality, integration ecosystem, and total cost of ownership. Zendesk scored at the top for ticketing depth and multi-channel coverage, competitive on integrations and reporting, and lower on AI (where Intercom's Fin leads) and pricing value (where Freshdesk offers more for less).

## **2. What is Zendesk? Understanding the Platform**

\[VISUAL: Zendesk platform diagram showing Support, Chat/Messaging, Talk, Guide, Gather, and Explore\]

Zendesk is a comprehensive customer service platform consisting of multiple products bundled as "Zendesk Suite": Support (ticketing), Messaging (live chat and chatbot), Talk (phone), Guide (knowledge base), Gather (community forums), and Explore (analytics). The Suite bundles all products into unified plans with a single Agent Workspace.

The Agent Workspace is where support agents spend their day. Every ticket—regardless of originating channel (email, chat, phone, social, web form)—appears in a unified queue. The workspace displays the customer's message thread alongside a context sidebar showing their full history: previous tickets, account information, satisfaction scores, and custom attributes. Agents respond without switching between tools or searching for customer information—the context is present automatically.

What distinguishes Zendesk from Intercom (messaging-centric) is the ticket model. Zendesk treats every customer interaction as a ticket with defined properties: status (new, open, pending, solved), priority (urgent, high, normal, low), type (question, incident, problem, task), assignee, SLA target, and custom fields. This structured model enables workflow automation (triggers, automations, macros) and reporting that messaging-centric platforms handle less formally.

What distinguishes Zendesk from Freshdesk (value-focused) is depth. Both handle ticketing, chat, phone, and knowledge base. But Zendesk's routing is more sophisticated, the trigger/automation engine is more powerful, the reporting goes deeper, and the enterprise features (multi-brand, advanced compliance, custom roles) serve larger organizations. Freshdesk provides 80% of Zendesk's capability at 50% of the cost—the question is whether the remaining 20% justifies the premium.

The multi-channel architecture is genuinely comprehensive. Email creates tickets automatically (our primary channel at 60% of volume). Chat enables real-time conversation through the web widget or in-app messaging. Talk provides cloud-based phone with IVR, recording, and analytics. Social connects Facebook, Twitter/X, Instagram, and WhatsApp. Web forms capture structured requests. And the API enables custom channels. All channels feed into the same Agent Workspace with unified customer history.

## **3. Zendesk Pricing & Plans: Enterprise Tier Structure**

\[VISUAL: Pricing comparison with feature gates between tiers\]

Zendesk Suite pricing gates significant features between tiers—understanding what each tier unlocks is essential for choosing the right plan.

### **3.1 Suite Team ($55/agent/month) - Entry Multi-Channel**

\[SCREENSHOT: Suite Team showing unified workspace with basic features\]

At $55/agent monthly (annual billing), Suite Team provides the unified Agent Workspace, email/chat/social/phone support, a basic AI chatbot, 1 help center, standard macros and views, and basic reporting. The entry point for multi-channel support.

Suite Team handles the fundamentals—tickets from any channel, agent assignment, basic automation. For small support teams (3-8 agents) with straightforward processes, Team provides a functional multi-channel help desk.

**Reality Check:** Suite Team lacks SLA management, custom ticket forms, and advanced routing—features that most growing support teams need within 3-6 months. The $55/agent pricing is in line with competitors (Freshdesk Pro at $49 includes similar capabilities), but the missing features push many teams to Growth quickly.

**What's Missing:** No SLA policies, no custom ticket forms, no business hours configuration, no multilingual support. These gaps mean Team is sufficient for basic support but insufficient for operations with service level commitments or complex ticket routing.

### **3.2 Suite Growth ($89/agent/month) - Operational Support**

\[SCREENSHOT: Suite Growth showing SLA management and custom ticket forms\]

At $89/agent monthly (annual), Suite Growth adds the features that transform Zendesk from "basic help desk" to "operational support platform": SLA policies with business hours and breach notifications, custom ticket forms (different forms for different request types), multilingual content support, satisfaction prediction (AI predicting whether a ticket will result in a bad satisfaction score), and multiple help centers for different brands or audiences.

This is where Zendesk becomes genuinely useful for growing support operations. The SLA management was the feature that differentiated our Growth experience from Team: defining "Enterprise customers get 1-hour first response, Standard customers get 4 hours" and tracking compliance automatically changed how we managed support quality.

**Best For:** Most support teams. Growth provides the SLA management, custom forms, and multilingual support that operational help desks require. Unless you need skills-based routing or HIPAA, Growth serves well.

### **3.3 Suite Professional ($115/agent/month) - Advanced Operations**

\[SCREENSHOT: Professional showing skills-based routing and custom analytics\]

At $115/agent monthly (annual), Professional adds skills-based routing (assigning tickets based on agent expertise), custom analytics (Explore custom reports and dashboards), side conversations (engaging other teams within a ticket without customer visibility), HIPAA eligibility, and advanced AI features.

The skills-based routing on Professional was the upgrade our team wanted most. Routing billing questions directly to billing-trained agents (instead of round-robin assignment) reduced average resolution time by 25%—agents received tickets they were qualified to handle rather than needing to transfer or escalate.

**Best For:** Support teams of 15+ agents with specialized expertise areas. The skills-based routing, custom analytics, and HIPAA eligibility serve organizations with complex operational needs.

### **3.4 Suite Enterprise (Custom) - Full Platform**

Enterprise adds custom roles, advanced AI-powered content cues, sandbox for testing, SLA with operational-level agreements, and dedicated support team. Enterprise pricing is custom and typically serves organizations with 50+ agents.

### **3.5 Advanced AI Add-On ($50/agent/month)**

\[VISUAL: Advanced AI cost breakdown showing per-agent add-on pricing\]

Zendesk's Advanced AI add-on ($50/agent/month on top of suite pricing) provides intent detection (classifying ticket topics automatically), sentiment analysis (flagging frustrated customers for priority handling), suggested macro responses, and AI-powered content cues for help center optimization.

The Advanced AI cost is significant—a 15-agent team adds $750/month on top of existing licensing. Compare this to Intercom's Fin AI, which is included in the base plan (with per-resolution pricing). The economics of Zendesk's AI approach (per-agent add-on) versus Intercom's (per-resolution) depend on your ticket volume and resolution patterns.

**Caution:** The Advanced AI add-on at $50/agent effectively makes Zendesk's AI-enhanced experience cost $105-165/agent/month (suite + AI). At this price point, evaluate carefully whether Intercom ($85/seat + $0.99/resolution) provides better AI value for your volume.

## **4. Key Features Deep Dive**

### **4.1 Ticketing System - The Gold Standard**

\[SCREENSHOT: Ticket view showing customer conversation, internal notes, ticket properties, and macros\]

Zendesk's ticketing system handles enterprise support complexity with a sophistication that no competitor fully matches. Tickets are created from any channel, assigned to agents or groups through configurable routing rules, tracked against SLA targets, and resolved through workflows that are completely customizable.

Our 15-person team processed 8,000+ tickets monthly. The routing rules automatically assigned tickets based on four criteria: channel (chat tickets route to the chat-specialized team), topic (billing questions detected by keywords route to billing specialists), language (Spanish-language tickets route to bilingual agents), and customer tier (enterprise customers route to senior agents with priority SLA). The routing eliminated 2 hours of daily manual triage that our previous system required.

Triggers fire when ticket conditions are met—creating the automation engine that powers Zendesk's operational efficiency. Our 35 triggers covered: auto-assignment (new tickets in the billing form → assign to billing group), priority escalation (ticket from enterprise customer → set priority to high), customer notification (ticket status changes to pending → send "waiting for information" email), agent alerts (ticket SLA approaching breach → send Slack notification to team lead), and data management (ticket solved for 30+ days → close automatically).

Macros (saved response templates) standardize agent replies for common scenarios. Our 40 macros covered every recurring ticket type: password reset instructions, billing inquiry responses, feature explanation templates, and troubleshooting step sequences. Each macro includes ticket property changes (set status, add tags, assign group) alongside the message text—so a single macro both responds to the customer and updates the ticket properties.

SLA policies (Growth and above) define response and resolution time targets per customer tier. We configured 4 SLA tiers: Enterprise (1-hour first response, 4-hour resolution), Professional (4-hour response, 1-business-day resolution), Standard (8-hour response, 2-business-day resolution), and Free (24-hour response, best-effort resolution). The SLA dashboard shows real-time compliance across all active tickets—highlighting tickets approaching breach before they actually breach.

**What's Missing:** The trigger and automation system is powerful but has a learning curve. Understanding the difference between triggers (fire when ticket is created or updated) and automations (fire on time-based conditions) requires reading documentation. Complex trigger interactions can produce unexpected results—a ticket update that fires multiple triggers in sequence can create confusing behavior. Test complex trigger configurations carefully.

### **4.2 Agent Workspace - Unified Multi-Channel Experience**

\[SCREENSHOT: Agent Workspace showing email ticket, customer context, and quick-access to chat and phone\]

The Agent Workspace consolidates all channels into a single queue with a unified interface. An agent handles an email ticket, accepts a live chat, takes a phone call—all from the same window. The customer context sidebar updates for each interaction, showing relevant history regardless of the channel the customer used previously.

Our team handles support across email (60% of volume), chat (25%), social media (10%), and phone (5%). The unified queue means agents don't need separate tools for each channel. The cross-channel customer history means a customer who emails on Monday and chats on Wednesday experiences continuity—the chat agent sees the email conversation and doesn't ask the customer to repeat their issue.

The workspace efficiency features that mattered most for our team: Views (saved ticket filters showing specific subsets—"my open tickets," "unassigned urgent," "enterprise pending"), Keyboard shortcuts (navigate tickets, change status, assign groups without mouse), and Side conversations (engage other teams within a ticket without the customer seeing the internal discussion—essential for escalation and collaboration).

### **4.3 Zendesk AI - Answer Bot and Advanced AI**

\[SCREENSHOT: AI chatbot suggesting help articles and Advanced AI showing intent detection\]

Zendesk's AI operates on two levels. The base AI (included in all Suite plans) provides Answer Bot—a chatbot that suggests help center articles based on customer questions. The Advanced AI (add-on at $50/agent/month) provides intent detection, sentiment analysis, macro suggestions, and intelligent triage.

Answer Bot resolved approximately 20% of incoming chat inquiries by suggesting relevant help articles. The mechanism is article suggestion rather than conversational resolution—the bot presents articles and asks "did this help?" which is less natural than Intercom's Fin (which answers questions conversationally). The resolution rate of 20% compares unfavorably with Fin's 40%+, though Answer Bot's simpler approach also creates fewer customer experience risks.

The Advanced AI's intent detection automatically classifies incoming tickets by topic: billing, technical_issue, feature_request, account_access, etc. The classification enables automated routing (technical issues → engineering support), prioritization (billing disputes → high priority), and reporting (what percentage of tickets are billing-related this month?). The intent detection accuracy was approximately 85% in our testing—good enough for routing automation, occasionally wrong enough to require manual correction.

Sentiment analysis flags tickets with negative sentiment for priority handling. During our evaluation, sentiment detection surfaced 15-20 frustrated customers per week who would have otherwise entered the normal queue. The early identification enabled proactive outreach that improved their satisfaction outcomes.

**Reality Check:** Zendesk's AI is functional but not transformative. Intercom's Fin resolves 40%+ of conversations conversationally; Zendesk's Answer Bot resolves 20% through article suggestion. The gap is significant—and Zendesk is investing heavily in AI to close it—but as of 2025, Intercom leads in AI-first customer support. However, Zendesk's AI is more integrated with the ticketing workflow (intent detection feeds routing rules, sentiment feeds priority), which serves operations-focused teams better than Intercom's conversation-level AI.

### **4.4 Zendesk Guide - Knowledge Base**

\[SCREENSHOT: Help center with articles organized by category, search bar, and community link\]

Guide provides a customizable knowledge base for self-service support. Articles organized by categories and sections, searchable with relevance ranking, and embeddable in the web widget for proactive self-service. The editor supports rich text, images, videos, and code blocks.

Our help center with 300+ articles handled approximately 40% of customer inquiries before they became tickets—customers finding answers through self-service search. The analytics show which articles are viewed most frequently, which searches return no results (indicating content gaps), and which articles customers view before submitting tickets (indicating articles that need improvement). These content analytics directly inform help center optimization.

The help center customization supports branding (custom themes matching your product's design), multiple help centers (different brands or product lines), and multilingual content (translated articles for international audiences). Our two help centers—one for the main product, one for the developer API—serve different audience segments with tailored content.

### **4.5 Zendesk Talk - Phone Support**

\[SCREENSHOT: Talk dashboard showing call queue, agent status, and call metrics\]

Talk provides built-in cloud phone support with IVR (Interactive Voice Response), call recording, voicemail, callback requests, and call routing. The integration with ticketing means phone calls automatically create tickets with recording attachments—the agent doesn't need to manually log the call.

Our phone support handles 400+ calls monthly. The IVR routes callers to the appropriate department ("Press 1 for billing, Press 2 for technical support"). Call recording captures conversations for quality assurance and dispute resolution. The callback feature lets customers request a return call instead of waiting on hold—reducing abandonment rates and improving customer experience.

Call analytics show queue wait times, abandonment rates, call durations, and agent performance. Our weekly review of call metrics identified that Tuesday afternoons had the longest wait times—we adjusted agent scheduling to add phone coverage during that period, reducing average wait time from 8 minutes to 3 minutes.

**What's Missing:** Talk's phone capabilities are solid but don't match dedicated contact center platforms (Five9, Talkdesk, Genesys) for complex call center operations—automatic call distribution algorithms, advanced workforce management, real-time speech analytics, and omnichannel routing intelligence. For basic-to-moderate phone support, Talk serves well. For organizations where phone is the primary support channel with 20+ agents, dedicated contact center platforms may serve better.

### **4.6 Zendesk Explore - Analytics**

\[SCREENSHOT: Explore dashboard showing ticket volume trends, SLA compliance, agent performance, and CSAT\]

Explore provides pre-built and custom reports across all Zendesk products. The analytics engine is comprehensive—covering ticket volume, response times, resolution times, SLA compliance, agent productivity, channel distribution, customer satisfaction trends, and knowledge base performance.

Our management team used three dashboards daily: the Operations Dashboard (real-time ticket volume, SLA compliance, queue depth, and active agents), the Performance Dashboard (agent productivity, average handle time, resolution rate, and quality scores), and the Customer Dashboard (satisfaction trends, recurring issue categories, and escalation rates). The dashboards served different stakeholders—the support manager used Operations for real-time decisions, the director used Performance for weekly reviews, and the VP used Customer for monthly reporting.

Custom reporting (Professional and above) enables analytics that pre-built reports don't cover. We built custom reports for: first-contact resolution rate by ticket category, average escalation time by customer tier, correlation between response time and satisfaction score, and agent handle time trends over training periods. The custom report builder uses a drag-and-drop metric/dimension model that's accessible to non-technical users—our support manager built reports without analytics training.

**What's Missing:** Explore is powerful for Zendesk-specific metrics but doesn't connect to external data sources (revenue data, product usage, marketing attribution). For cross-functional analytics that span support, sales, and product, organizations need to export Zendesk data to a BI tool (Looker, Tableau, Power BI).

### **4.7 Marketplace - 1,500+ Apps**

\[SCREENSHOT: Zendesk Marketplace showing popular apps by category\]

The Zendesk Marketplace is the largest app ecosystem in customer support—1,500+ apps covering CRM integration (Salesforce, HubSpot), productivity (time tracking, agent coaching), quality assurance (Klaus, MaestroQA), workforce management (Tymeshift, Assembled), and channel extensions (WhatsApp, Instagram).

Our 12 installed apps included Slack (ticket notifications in support channels), Salesforce (CRM data in the customer context sidebar), Klaus (quality scoring for agent performance), Productboard (feature request tracking from tickets), and SurveyMonkey (post-resolution surveys with deeper questions than Zendesk's built-in CSAT).

The marketplace quality varies. First-party apps (built by Zendesk) and popular third-party apps (Salesforce, Slack) are well-maintained and reliable. Smaller third-party apps occasionally have compatibility issues after Zendesk updates. Before installing an app, check the last update date, user reviews, and support responsiveness.

## **6. Zendesk Cons: The Honest Pain Points**

\[VISUAL: Cons summary infographic highlighting main pain points\]

### **Configuration Complexity Requires Dedicated Administration**

Configuring triggers, automations, SLA policies, routing rules, views, macros, and ticket forms requires ongoing administrative attention. Our deployment needed 3 weeks of initial configuration and approximately 5 hours per week of ongoing admin work. For organizations without a dedicated Zendesk admin (part-time for under 20 agents, full-time for 20+), the platform's power goes underutilized.

### **AI Trails Intercom**

Answer Bot resolves 20% of conversations through article suggestions. Intercom's Fin resolves 40%+ through conversational AI. The gap in AI capability is real and significant for organizations where AI resolution is a strategic priority. The Advanced AI add-on ($50/agent) narrows the gap but adds substantial cost.

### **Pricing Escalates With Feature Gates**

The $55 entry price is misleading for most operations. SLA management requires $89/agent. Skills-based routing requires $115/agent. Advanced AI requires +$50/agent. A "fully capable" Zendesk deployment costs $115-165/agent—two to three times the entry price.

### **Agent Interface Could Be More Modern**

The Agent Workspace has improved significantly but still feels enterprise-grade rather than consumer-grade. Compared to Intercom's clean messaging interface or Help Scout's email-like simplicity, Zendesk's workspace is information-dense and visually heavy. New agents take longer to feel comfortable, and daily satisfaction scores are lower than simpler alternatives.

### **Setup Complexity for Full Capability**

Reaching Zendesk's full potential requires 2-4 weeks of configuration. Simple tools (Crisp, Help Scout) are productive in hours. The configuration investment is justified for complex operations but excessive for simple support needs.

### **Per-Agent Pricing Scales Linearly**

A 30-agent team on Professional costs $3,450/month. A 50-agent team costs $5,750/month. Adding agents increases costs directly. For large support teams, the per-agent model creates budget pressure—especially when combined with AI add-on costs.

**Caution:** Before evaluating Zendesk, determine which tier you'll actually need. Most teams need Growth ($89) or Professional ($115), not Team ($55). Budget and evaluate at the realistic tier, not the entry price.

## **7. Setup & Implementation**

\[VISUAL: Implementation timeline infographic\]

### **The Real Timeline**

**Week 1: Foundation**

Create your Zendesk instance, configure email channel (connect your support email addresses), set up the web widget for chat, and configure basic views and macros. Invite your team with appropriate roles. The basic help desk is functional within days—tickets arrive, agents respond.

**Week 2: Customization**

Configure ticket forms for different request types (billing inquiry, technical support, feature request—each with appropriate fields). Build routing rules based on form type, customer attributes, and channel. Create SLA policies with business hours. Set up trigger automations for the most repetitive manual tasks.

**Week 3: Knowledge Base and AI**

Create or migrate help center articles organized by category. Configure Answer Bot with your content. Set up the web widget to show relevant articles proactively. Build agent-facing macros for common response scenarios.

**Week 4: Reporting and Optimization**

Build management dashboards in Explore. Configure satisfaction surveys. Set up Slack or email notifications for SLA breaches. Train the team on the complete workflow—ticket handling, macro usage, SLA awareness.

**Month 2+: Refinement**

Review ticket routing accuracy and adjust rules. Analyze SLA compliance and identify improvement areas. Add marketplace apps for specific needs. Refine trigger automations based on operational patterns. Consider Advanced AI add-on based on volume and routing needs.

**Pro Tip:** Resist the urge to configure everything in week one. Start with basic routing and simple triggers. Run for 2-3 weeks with basic configuration, then customize based on the actual patterns you observe. Over-configuring before you have data leads to rules that don't match real ticket patterns.

## **8. Zendesk vs Competitors: Detailed Comparisons**

\[VISUAL: Competitor logos arranged in versus format\]

### **Zendesk vs Intercom: Ticket-Centric vs Messaging-Centric**

This is the most important comparison in customer support platforms.

**Where Intercom Wins:** Superior AI chatbot (Fin resolves 42% vs Answer Bot's 20%), better proactive messaging (in-app targeting based on user behavior), more modern conversational customer experience, and richer customer context sidebar. Intercom transforms support from tickets to conversations.

**Where Zendesk Wins:** More structured ticket management with sophisticated SLA workflows, built-in phone support (Talk), larger app marketplace (1,500+), more mature enterprise features (multi-brand, advanced compliance, custom roles), better for organizations with complex, multi-team support processes. Zendesk handles operational support complexity that Intercom doesn't attempt.

**Choose Intercom if:** Your support is messaging-centric, you want the best AI chatbot, you serve a SaaS product with in-app support needs, or proactive communication is strategic.

**Choose Zendesk if:** Your support is ticket-centric across email/chat/phone/social, you need structured SLA management, you require built-in phone support, or you need the most comprehensive enterprise features.

### **Zendesk vs Freshdesk: Enterprise vs Value**

**Where Freshdesk Wins:** 50-60% cheaper at comparable feature levels ($49/agent Pro vs Zendesk's $89 Growth for similar capabilities). Free plan for 2 agents. Simpler administration. Better value-per-dollar for budget-conscious support teams.

**Where Zendesk Wins:** Deeper ticketing features (more sophisticated triggers, better SLA management). Larger marketplace (1,500+ vs Freshdesk's ~800). Stronger phone support (Talk vs Freshdesk's Contact Center). More mature enterprise governance. Better reporting depth.

**Choose Freshdesk if:** Budget is the primary constraint and you need competent multi-channel support. Freshdesk provides 80% of Zendesk's capability at half the cost.

**Choose Zendesk if:** You need the deepest ticketing features, the largest app ecosystem, or the most mature enterprise compliance.

### **Zendesk vs Help Scout: Enterprise vs Simplicity**

**Where Help Scout Wins:** Dramatically simpler experience designed for email-first support. Every customer interaction feels like a personal email. Faster agent adoption. Lower cost ($20-65/user). Better for small teams wanting beautiful simplicity.

**Where Zendesk Wins:** Multi-channel breadth (phone, social, chat + email). SLA management. Complex routing. Enterprise governance. Custom analytics. Better for organizations with operational complexity.

**Choose Help Scout if:** Your support is primarily email-based, your team values simplicity, and you have under 15 agents.

**Choose Zendesk if:** You need multi-channel support, SLA management, or enterprise-grade operations.

## **9. Best Use Cases & Industries**

\[VISUAL: Industry icons with use case highlights\]

### **Enterprise Multi-Channel Support - Perfect Fit**

Organizations with 15+ agents handling email, chat, phone, and social need the channel breadth and operational depth that only Zendesk (and, to a lesser extent, Freshdesk) provides. Our 15-agent team across 4 channels found Zendesk uniquely capable of unifying the experience.

**Key Success Factors:** Configure routing rules for each channel, build SLA policies by customer tier, create channel-specific macros, and use Explore dashboards for operational visibility.

### **Regulated Industries (Healthcare, Finance) - Perfect Fit**

On Professional ($115/agent) and above with specific configuration and BAA. Team and Growth are not HIPAA eligible.
